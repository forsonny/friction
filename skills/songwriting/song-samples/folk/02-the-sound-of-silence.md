# The Sound of Silence

**Artist:** Simon & Garfunkel
**Album:** Wednesday Morning, 3 A.M. (1964)
**Genre:** Folk rock

---

## Structure Analysis

**Structure Type:** Address to abstraction

| Section | Lines | Purpose |
|---------|-------|---------|
| Verse 1 | 6 | Greeting darkness |
| Verse 2 | 6 | Vision description |
| Verse 3 | 6 | People not communicating |
| Verse 4 | 6 | Warning ignored |
| Verse 5 | 6 | Neon god worship |

**Why this structure works:**
Continuous verses build prophetic vision of modern alienation.

---

## Opening Technique

**Technique:** 6 (Address to Abstraction)

**Opening line(s):**
> "Hello darkness, my old friend, I've come to talk with you again"

**Why it works:**
Personifies darkness. "Old friend" suggests familiar relationship with isolation. Immediately intimate and strange.

---

## Hook Analysis

**The hook:**
> "The sound of silence"

**Syllable count:** 5

**Conversational test:** Fail - But oxymoron is memorable

**Why it works:**
- Paradox demands attention
- Silence having sound = emptiness that speaks
- Title earworm despite being abstract

---

## Notable Lyrical Techniques

### 1. Personification of darkness
**Example:**
> "Hello darkness, my old friend"

**Why it works:** Makes isolation a relationship, not just a state

### 2. Oxymoron as concept
**Example:**
> "Sound of silence" - hearing nothing

**Why it works:** Contradiction captures modern alienation

### 3. Neon god
**Example:**
> "Bowed and prayed to the neon god they made"

**Why it works:** Commercial worship as religion

## "Said Not Written" Lines

Lines that sound like real speech:

1. > "I've come to talk with you again"
   - Sounds said because: Greeting to familiar presence

2. > "Fools, said I, you do not know"
   - Sounds said because: Exasperation, direct speech

3. > "Hear my words that I might teach you"
   - Sounds said because: Prophet's plea

2. Oxymorons make memorable hooks
